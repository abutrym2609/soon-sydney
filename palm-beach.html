Palm Beach is a rare double-sided suburb. You can watch the sunrise over the Pacific Ocean, then walk 500 metres across the isthmus to watch the sunset over the calm waters of Pittwater. Two different bodies of water. One peninsula. No traffic lights — a deliberate community decision to maintain the feeling that you are officially off the clock.

"Palmy" is globally famous as Summer Bay from the soap opera Home and Away — filmed here continuously since 1988. It is common to see film crews and Alf Stewart fans mingling with tech CEOs on the same stretch of sand. The suburb that is simultaneously a billionaire's hideout and a TV set has never been asked to resolve this contradiction.

The community actively resists infrastructure improvements — better roads, expanded parking, increased bus frequency — that would make Palm Beach easier for others to visit. The wealthy hermit vibe is not incidental. It is engineered, maintained, and defended by people who understand exactly what they are protecting and why access should remain inconvenient.

Walking along Ocean Road feels like stepping into a high-end travel magazine. The architecture is a mix of weathered timber beach shacks (worth millions) and brutalist concrete masterpieces tucked into the hillside. There is no traditional high street. Commerce clusters in pockets — chic boutiques and florist-styled cafes that smell of salt and expensive espresso.

The rhythm is intensely seasonal. At 6am, the Dawn Patrol of surfers and lighthouse hikers dominates. By midday on weekends, the area swells with day-trippers. By Sunday night, a profound and expensive hush falls over the peninsula. The Boathouse defines the aesthetic. Jonah's defines the ambition.

 

The great paradox of Palm Beach: people pay $10M+ for homes here to be "seen" in one of the world's most beautiful spots, yet the community fiercely fights any infrastructure that would make it easier for others to arrive. The end of the road where Sydney's elite trade their suits for linen and surfboards — framed by the iconic sweep of Barrenjoey Headland.

The suburb is shifting from a weekend-only destination to a primary residence hub for ultra-wealthy remote workers. Year-round demand for high-end hospitality is rising. The 90-minute commute to the city is considered the price of paradise by people who are not required to make the journey often.

Who lives here

The Legacy Blue-Blood

Multi-Generational Families

Owned a weekender here for three generations. Value privacy and the "old Palmy" rustic charm before the glass boxes arrived. The founding conscience of the community — suspicious of every planning application and correct to be so.

The Coastal Creative

Designers, Architects & Media

Work remotely, prioritise the aesthetic inspiration of the ocean, and have arranged their professional lives so that the 41km from the CBD is irrelevant on most days. The lighthouse before the first Zoom call is non-negotiable.

The Sea-Change CEO

Ultra-High Net Worth Residents

Traded the Vaucluse mansion for a permanent Pittwater lifestyle. Commute via seaplane or private car. Have enough control over their schedules that the 90-minute drive to the city happens on their terms, infrequently.

The Surf Purist

Local Families

Lives revolve entirely around Palm Beach SLSC and the swell forecast. In the few remaining pockets of relative affordability. Watch the glass boxes multiply with the particular feeling of someone watching their suburb be slowly replaced by its own reputation.

Life in Palm Beach

2026
01

Barrenjoey Road

The spine — quiet and functional on Tuesday mornings, a slow-moving parade of luxury SUVs and tourists by Saturday afternoon. The same street, two entirely different suburbs depending on the day. Locals navigate by calendar rather than geography.

02

Food & Drink

Every day — sourdough and flat whites at Dunes, the ritual that precedes the lighthouse hike. The occasion — a long, champagne-soaked lunch at Barrenjoey House or Jonah's, perched on the cliff with the Hawkesbury in the background, where the bill is irrelevant and the view is the point.

03

Barrenjoey Headland

The premier hike — 360-degree views of the Hawkesbury River, the Pacific Ocean, Pittwater, and the Lion Island Nature Reserve. The lighthouse at the top, restored in 2026, standing where it has stood since 1881. The walk that justifies the entire decision to live here.

04

Pittwater Life

Sailing out of Palm Beach Sailing Club. Sundowners on a Pittwater-facing deck. The ferry to Wagstaffe for lunch. The calm, enclosed water that makes Palm Beach fundamentally different from every other ocean suburb — you are never more than five minutes from stillness.

05

SLSC & Dawn Patrol

Palm Beach Surf Life Saving Club is the social and athletic hub — surf boat rowing, ironman training, and the annual Pittwater to Coogee ocean swim. At 6am, the beach belongs to surfers and lighthouse hikers. This is considered the correct order of events.

06

2026 Update

The Barrenjoey Lighthouse Restoration finalised — improved accessibility, preserved 19th-century stonework. Governor Phillip Park upgrades complete — refined amenities for slow tourism visitors. Coastal Walkway Palm Beach to Whale Beach in final planning — the pedestrian infrastructure the narrow bends have needed for decades.

How they spend their time

2026
01

The Lighthouse Loop

Approximately 3km return — the daily cardio ritual for Palm Beach locals. Ends at the lighthouse with 360-degree views. Conducted before 8am. Everything after is considered the rest of the day.

02

Surfing

"The Kiddies" and the North End — two different breaks for two different states of mind. The former: gentle, sociable, appropriate for all. The latter: powerful, territorial, for those who have earned their position in the lineup.

03

Sailing on Pittwater

Palm Beach Sailing Club. Private vessels moored in front of waterfront homes. The calm, sheltered water of Pittwater as the antidote to the exposed ocean beach 500 metres away. Two environments. One suburb. Neither optional.

04

Beach Yoga & Pilates

Sunrise yoga on the beach. Private Pilates sessions. The wellness culture of Palm Beach is conducted quietly, outdoors, and without the commercial infrastructure that surrounds it in the city. This is considered a feature.

05

Plein-Air & Photography

Artists frequently set up on the headland or the Pittwater foreshore. Pop-up galleries in nearby Avalon. The visual beauty of the peninsula is not incidental to life here — it is the primary resource, used daily and never depleted.

06

Pittwater Sundowners

Drinks on a west-facing deck or the bow of a boat as the sun drops over Pittwater. The non-negotiable end to the day — practised year-round, in every season, by residents who arranged their lives specifically to be present for this moment.

2026 Update

From Weekend Retreat to Primary Residence

Palm Beach is completing a quiet transformation — from seasonal destination to permanent home for ultra-wealthy remote workers. Year-round demand for high-end hospitality is rising. The infrastructure that once felt excessive for a weekend population is now simply necessary.

Barrenjoey Lighthouse — Restored
Restoration finalised — improved accessibility with preserved 19th-century stonework. The most significant heritage milestone in Palm Beach in a generation.
Governor Phillip Park — Complete
Park upgrades complete — refined amenities for slow tourism, environmental protection maintained. The park that defines Palm Beach's public identity, now meeting its private standards.
Coastal Walkway — Planning
Palm Beach to Whale Beach pedestrian link in final planning — improved safety on narrow coastal bends. The infrastructure that every resident walks and every planner discusses.
