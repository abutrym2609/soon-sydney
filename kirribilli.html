Two official government residences — Admiralty House (Governor-General) and Kirribilli House (Prime Minister). The nation's secondary political hub.

Despite its ultra-wealthy reputation, over 50% of residents live alone — primarily in heritage-listed Art Deco apartment blocks rather than mansions.

Name derived from the Aboriginal word Kiarabilli — "good fishing spot." One of Sydney's oldest inhabited places, now home to the most expensive views in the city.

A walk down Broughton Street feels like stepping into a curated European village that happens to have the world's most famous bridge looming over it. The atmosphere is hushed but high-energy — a dense, beautiful mix of Victorian terraces, Federation-style homes, and Art Deco apartment blocks.

Mornings belong to the Active Elite — locals in designer activewear grabbing a flat white before a commute that involves a ferry rather than a freeway.

 

The great contradiction of Kirribilli is its density versus exclusivity. One of the most densely populated suburbs in Australia, yet it maintains an aura of extreme privacy. You are minutes from the CBD, but the only sound you often hear is water slapping against the bridge pylons.

Weekends transform the area — Kirribilli Markets bring bohemian flair, while families flock to Bradfield Park for picnics with what may be the finest urban backdrop on earth.

Life in Kirribilli

2026
01

Broughton Street

The heartbeat — compact, bustling. By day a parade of gourmet delis and florists. By night, intimate wine bars and Italian trattorias.

02

Two Dining Cultures

Everyday — Batch Burgers, a good coffee, nothing fussy. Upscale — Sails or The Kirribilli Club with panoramic harbour views. The gap between them is significant.

03

The Boardwalk

Kirribilli to Lavender Bay — flat, scenic, past Luna Park and Wendy Whiteley's Secret Garden. The suburb's outdoor living room.

04

No-Car Ethos

Real residents rarely drive. The ferry is the limousine of the people. The train from Milsons Point is one of the city's most frequent lines.

05

Cultural Life

The Kirribilli Markets (Art, Design & Fashion) maintain a creative edge in an otherwise conservative suburb. Fortnightly. Always crowded.

06

2026 Update

Milsons Point Station upgrade completed late 2025 — full lift access, modernised entry. Several new micro-bars opened in hidden lanes behind Broughton Street.

How they spend their time

2026
01

Sailing

Sydney Flying Squadron — local go-to for harbour sailing and harbourside drinks after.

02

The Bridge Loop

Run across the Harbour Bridge to the CBD and back via ferry. ~4–5km. Best commute in the world.

03

Wellness

Yoga on the grass at Jeffrey Street Wharf at sunrise. The dominant local aesthetic — unhurried, deliberate.

04

Trivia Circuit

The Kirribilli Hotel trivia — surprisingly competitive. Lawyers and political staffers. Do not take it lightly.

05

Jacaranda Season

November. The entire suburb turns purple. Tourists block the streets. Locals both love and resent it equally.

06

The Sunset Sip

Mary Booth Lookout, a bottle of wine, watching the CBD lights turn on. A Kirribilli ritual with no substitute.

2026 Update

Younger, but Even More Exclusive

Kirribilli is shifting. The influx of high-earning professionals in their 30s is moving the vibe from stuffy heritage to sophisticated harbourside chic. The infrastructure is catching up — and the prices are following.

Milsons Point Station — Complete
Fully upgraded late 2025 — lift access, modernised entry. The gateway to the suburb finally matches its reputation.
Micro-Bars — Open
Several new small bars in the hidden lanes behind Broughton Street, shifting the evening energy from conservative to curated.
Harbour Shadows — Coming
High-end boutique residential on Holbrook Avenue nearing completion — a new wave of ultra-wealthy residents on the way.
