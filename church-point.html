Church Point serves as the primary mainland hub for the offshore residents of Scotland Island, Lovett Bay, and Elvina Bay. Its car park is one of the few places in Sydney where boat trailers outnumber regular cars — a logistical fact that describes the suburb more precisely than any marketing copy has managed.

Despite its small size, Church Point has one of the highest concentrations of boat ownership per capita in Australia. The suburb treats the water as a transport network rather than a leisure amenity. The clinking of yacht masts replaces the ambient noise of traffic. Both are background noise. Only one is relaxing.

McCarrs Creek Road winds through Ku-ring-gai Chase National Park to reach Church Point, creating a natural barrier against through-traffic. The road ends here. This is not a design choice — it is topography. The consequence is an eerie quiet that the suburb has had for decades and intends to keep. Parking is the single most discussed lifestyle topic in the area.

A walk through Church Point isn't along a high street — it's along a wooden boardwalk. No city centre. Only the Pasadena and the Church Point Waterfront Store. The architecture is a mix of sprawling multi-million dollar glass pavilions perched on the hillside and rustic timber boatsheds at the water's edge. The contrast is not managed. It has simply arrived at this configuration and remained.

In the morning, a focused energy as offshore residents dock their tinnies and rush to their cars. By afternoon: clinking wine glasses, the smell of salt spray, and the particular hush of a suburb where the road has ended and there is nowhere further to go.

 

The paradox: one of Sydney's most prestigious addresses lacks basic urban amenities — no supermarket, no train station, no pharmacy you can "pop out" to. Isolated luxury. It feels like a high-end holiday resort year-round, buzzing with boaties in the sun and turning entirely silent after dark. Every dog's name is known here. This is not unusual. It is the point.

Church Point is where Sydney's northern pulse slows to a nautical rhythm. The suburb is becoming slightly more commercialised as a weekend destination — shifting from sleepy secret to polished maritime lifestyle hub. The residents have noticed. They are managing their feelings about it.

Who lives here

The Maritime Aristocracy

Wealthy Retirees & Executives

Own large yachts. Want to wake up overlooking their mooring. Found in Church Point a suburb where that sentence is not aspirational but operational. The boat is in front of the house. The house is designed around the view of the boat.

The Offshore Commuter

Scotland Island & Lovett Bay Residents

Live offshore but anchor their mainland life here — car parked at the Point, commute by tinnie. Use Church Point as the decompression point between the island and the city. The ferry crossing is the transition, and it is considered the best part of the journey.

The Creative Seclusionist

Artists & Writers

Drawn to the stillness and the dramatic light reflecting off Pittwater. Found in Church Point the quiet that their work requires at a slightly lower logistical cost than the offshore suburbs. Still require a boat. Consider this proportionate.

The Nature Enthusiast

Active Families

Prioritise proximity to Ku-ring-gai Chase National Park and water sports over shopping malls. Have confirmed that the 20-minute drive to Mona Vale for groceries is acceptable in exchange for the view from every window. Review this assessment annually. Confirm it each time.

Life in Church Point

2026
01

The Waterfront Precinct

The boardwalk — the entire commercial and social infrastructure of Church Point compressed into 200 metres of waterfront. The Waterfront Store for coffee and gourmet deli goods; The Pasadena for Mediterranean seafood with sunset views. Quality over quantity. The quantity is, objectively, insufficient. Nobody leaves.

02

Food & Drink

Every day — The Church Point Waterfront Store: coffee, gourmet supplies, the morning news exchanged with someone whose boat is better than yours. The occasion — Pasadena: revived boutique hotel and dining, Mediterranean-inspired seafood, sunset views over Pittwater that justify the decision to live here entirely.

03

The Ferry Hub

The operational heart of the Pittwater offshore communities. Scotland Island ferry. Lovett Bay water taxi. The morning departure of tinnies and the afternoon return. The 2026 Pittwater Commuter Wharf upgrade added more berths and improved boarding for elderly residents. The Church Point shuffle — finding a parking spot — remains unsolved.

04

McCarrs Creek Walk

The route through Ku-ring-gai Chase toward the national park — 5 to 10km, shaded, scenic, used by residents who live next to a national park and consider it entirely normal that their walk to nowhere in particular is world-class. The walk that justifies the 32km from the CBD.

05

Holmeport Marina

The Bayview Yacht Racing Association nearby. Kayaking and sailing from the Church Point Charter. The water as infrastructure — used for commuting, exercise, competition, and leisure simultaneously by different people at different hours, with no management required and no conflict recorded.

06

2026 Update

The Pittwater Commuter Wharf upgrade complete — more berths, improved elderly ferry access. Seawall & Boardwalk expansion done: improved pedestrian safety, more public lounging space. Electric Shuttle Bus feasibility study announced: direct connection to B-Line at Mona Vale. The parking problem may, one day, have a solution.

How they spend their time

2026
01

Kayaking & Sailing

Church Point Charter launches. The Bayview Yacht Racing Association. Morning paddles across to Scotland Island. The water that is outside every door, used for exercise, competition, and the particular form of leisure that requires you to pay attention to the wind.

02

McCarrs Creek Walk

The national park walk that starts at the road's end and goes into bush for as long as the walker decides. Shaded. Quiet. No coffee shop at the turnaround point. This is considered a feature by the people who walk it regularly.

03

Morning Yoga

On the Pasadena deck or private docks. The stillness of Pittwater at 7am as the appropriate setting for a practice that claims to value stillness. Church Point provides the stillness without irony, which is rarer than it sounds.

04

Rowing & Surf Boats

Local surf boat culture training at Mona Vale and Bayview. The team sport of a suburb where the water is the primary recreational infrastructure and the team members have all, in their own ways, organised their lives around being near it.

05

Church Point Social Club

Trivia nights and community talks at the local hall. The intellectual and social life of a suburb with a population of 1,100 and the community cohesion of something significantly smaller. Every dog's name is known here. Trivia questions about local geography are considered unfair.

06

Pittwater Sundowners

A glass of wine on a balcony or boat deck as the sun sets behind the hills of the national park. The non-negotiable end to the day at Church Point — conducted by residents who moved here specifically for this view and have confirmed, each evening, that the decision was correct.

Resident Voices

01 — Yachtie
Grant, 58
Loves it
I can be on my boat in three minutes from my front door. The serenity is unmatched. I have tested this claim extensively over fifteen years and found no evidence against it.
Frustrates
Weekend tourists who take all the parking spots just to take a photo of the view. I understand the impulse. I find the consequence unacceptable. I am not sure how to resolve this contradiction.
02 — Offshore Local
Maya, 34
Loves it
Living on Scotland Island and commuting via Church Point makes me feel alive. The crossing changes the day. I arrive at work having already done something remarkable before 9am.
Frustrates
The grocery haul from Mona Vale back to the ferry wharf when it's raining. I have a system. The system involves a trolley, waterproof bags, and a very specific set of timing decisions. It works. I wish I didn't need it.
03 — Empty Nester
Susan, 65
Loves it
It's like living in a postcard. We know every dog's name here. That sentence is not an exaggeration. I have confirmed it by trying to think of a dog I don't know the name of. I cannot.
Frustrates
The lack of a local pharmacy. You can't just pop out for medicine. You prepare. You stock. You plan. This is the correct way to live if you can manage it. Managing it takes practice.

2026 Update

From Sleepy Secret to Maritime Hub

Church Point is becoming slightly more commercialised as a weekend destination. The polished maritime lifestyle hub that it is becoming is not entirely different from the sleepy secret it was. The boats are the same. The prices are higher. The car park is still the problem.

Pittwater Wharf — Upgraded
More berths, improved elderly ferry boarding — the infrastructure that makes Church Point functional as a transport hub rather than merely beautiful as a location.
Seawall & Boardwalk — Complete
Improved pedestrian safety, more public lounging space by the water — formalising the waterfront as a civic amenity rather than merely a functional maritime precinct.
Electric Shuttle Bus — Feasibility
Study announced: direct connection to B-Line at Mona Vale. If built, the first public transport link that doesn't require a boat. The parking problem may have a solution by 2028.
